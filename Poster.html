AI Tools in Cross-Border Contracting:
Liability and Risk Allocation in International Business

"How should international businesses allocate liability when AI tools are used to draft, review, translate, or negotiate cross-border commercial contracts?"

Part I: The Setup (Context & Workflow)

Introduction

AI tools are increasingly used for contract drafting, review, translation, and negotiation. In cross-border contracts, mistakes have higher consequences due to varying legal systems.

The Legal Gap: Traditional commercial law relies on human intent; AI generation blurs the lines of agency and accountability in cross-border agreements.

The central issue is liability: who bears the risk if the AI-generated contract text is wrong?

AI-Assisted Contracting

  • Drafting clauses (e.g., limitation of liability)
  • Reviewing contracts (identifying non-standard terms)
  • Summarizing obligations (danger of omitting critical conditions)
  • Translating legal terms (high risk of nuance loss)
  • Comparing contract versions (overlooking subtle redlines)

Case Study: AI Translation Error in a German–Spanish Sales Contract

Scenario: A German company sells industrial components to a Spanish buyer. The seller uses an AI tool to translate and draft the English contract. The AI mistranslates a delivery obligation and incorrectly summarizes the agreed Incoterm, creating uncertainty about when risk passes. During transport, goods are damaged. Both parties claim the other side bears the risk.

Legal Questions:

  • Was the AI-generated wording part of the contract?
  • Which party bears the risk of the mistranslation?
  • Does the governing law or arbitration clause determine where the dispute is resolved?

Legal Resolution:

  • The Trap (CISG Art. 8): The AI's mistranslated terms are legally binding on the German seller because the Spanish buyer reasonably relied on them.
  • The Hammer (UNIDROIT Art. 3.2.2): The German seller cannot void the contract. Using an automated AI tool for a commercial contract without human legal review constitutes gross negligence. The seller is fully liable.
Part III: The Solution (Frameworks & Strategies)

How Existing International Commercial Law Addresses AI Contract Risk

CISG

International Sale of Goods

  • Interpretation of Intent (Art. 8): AI-generated text is legally binding if the receiving party reasonably relies on it.
  • Passing of Risk (Arts. 66–67): If an AI hallucinates or mistranslates an Incoterm (e.g., confusing EXW with DAP), it drastically alters when risk and costs transfer from seller to buyer, forcing courts to apply default CISG risk rules.
  • Exemption from Liability (Art. 79): Using unverified AI is a business choice completely within a party's control; it does not qualify as an unforeseeable impediment and offers no liability shield.

UNIDROIT Principles

General Contract Principles

  • Linguistic Discrepancies (Art. 4.7): In conflicts between document versions, the originally drafted language governs, stripping authority from the AI-translated text.
  • Error in Transmission (Art. 3.2.3): Mistakes generated by an automated translation tool are legally attributed to the sender. The sender bears the risk.
  • Gross Negligence Exception (Art. 3.2.2): A party cannot void a contract due to an error if they were grossly negligent. Deploying AI for technical cross-border contracts without mandatory human review constitutes gross negligence.

Contractual Strategies Checklist

  • Mandatory Human-in-the-Loop review Ensure human validation before contract transmission.
  • Define AI-use permissions Explicitly state if, how, and which AI tools can be utilized.
  • Language priority clause Determine which language version governs in case of discrepancies.
  • Clearly defined Incoterms Avoid ambiguous summaries of standard delivery/risk terms.
  • Governing law and arbitration clauses Secure the jurisdiction for dispute resolution.
  • Maintain audit trails Keep records of AI prompts, outputs, and drafts.

"Existing international commercial law already provides mechanisms to manage AI-related contractual risk. AI can accelerate international contracting, but liability remains strictly human and contractual."
